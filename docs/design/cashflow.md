# Cashflow design reflections

Notes toward a future cashflow specification for `jact`. This is a
design discussion, not an implementation spec. The goal is to pin down
the conceptual split between probability calculation and cashflow
calculation before committing to APIs or kernel changes.

**No active users yet.** Breaking changes are still cheap. This is the
right point to choose the clean model rather than preserve accidental
behaviour.

---

## 1. What cashflow is in this solver

The current solver computes a probability state over time:

- an absolutely continuous state-duration density per reachable state,
- plus a per-state point mass that follows the initial characteristic.

Cashflow is not a separate stochastic process in the same sense. It is
an **integral transform** of that probability state, possibly weighted
by intensities and possibly sampled at scheduled clock times.

At a high level there are three distinct sources of expected cashflow:

1. **State-rate cashflows.**
   Continuous payments while the policy is in a state, such as
   premiums, annuities, disability income, or expenses.
2. **Transition-triggered lump sums.**
   Payments caused by a transition `i -> j`, such as a death benefit or
   an onset benefit.
3. **Scheduled deterministic lump sums.**
   Payments at known clock times, possibly conditional on the state (and
   possibly duration) occupied at that time.

The first two fit naturally into the usual actuarial decomposition:

- State-rate component:
  `sum_i integral p_i(t, d) c_i(t, d) dd`, plus the point-mass term.
- Transition-lump component:
  `sum_{i != j} integral p_i(t, d) mu_ij(t, d) b_ij(t, d) dd`, plus the
  point-mass term.

The third component is different. A scheduled deterministic lump sum is
not generated by an intensity and is not tied to an edge in the state
graph. It is an event in **clock time**. That distinction should remain
explicit in the future API.

---

## 2. Why deterministic lump sums should not be forced into the payment matrix

The old prototype's payment-matrix idea is a good fit for:

- diagonal entries as state-rate cashflows,
- off-diagonal entries as transition-triggered lump sums.

It is not a good fit for deterministic scheduled payments.

A scheduled payment at time `tau` is conceptually:

- duration-insensitive:
  `sum_i P_i(tau) * ell_i(tau)`
- duration-sensitive:
  `sum_i integral p_i(tau, d) ell_i(tau, d) dd`, plus the point-mass
  contribution

That object depends on the probability state at a known time, not on an
intensity-mediated flow during a step. If it is squeezed into the
transition-payment abstraction, the model becomes less clear:

- users have to pretend a time event is an edge event,
- off-grid timing becomes harder to reason about,
- the distinction between "payment because a transition happened" and
  "payment because the calendar says so" disappears.

The cleaner mental model is:

- state-rate cashflows live on states,
- transition cashflows live on edges and are weighted by intensities,
- deterministic lump sums live on an event schedule in clock time.

---

## 3. Deterministic lump sums: preferred first direction

The simplest useful first direction is:

- fixed, declared event times,
- event times restricted to the solver grid,
- payments allowed to depend on current state,
- payments optionally allowed to depend on duration in current state.

The grid-aligned restriction is important. It avoids immediate pressure
to alter the stepping scheme just to support cashflow events.

With that restriction, an event at time `tau` can be evaluated directly
from the recorded or internal probability state at `tau`. The remaining
convention to settle later is whether the payment sees the state "just
before" or "just after" the step landing on `tau`. That timing issue is
real, but it is narrower and more manageable than supporting arbitrary
off-grid event times from day one.

Future work may still want:

- arbitrary event times,
- step splitting around event times,
- explicit left/right event conventions.

But those should be treated as a second-phase design problem, not the
baseline.

## 4. Recording semantics: probability snapshots vs cashflow accumulation

This is the main place where cashflow may want different semantics from
probability.

For probability, the current meaning of `record_every` is naturally:

- perform `record_every` inner steps,
- record the callback at the resulting endpoint.

That is a **snapshot** interpretation. It fits probability because the
user usually wants the state at specific times.

Cashflow is different. In many cases, the quantity users want is not an
instantaneous amount at the record time but the **sum of contributions
over the period since the previous record**.

That suggests three distinct recording semantics for future cashflow
callbacks:

1. **Point sampling**
   Record the expected cashflow at the endpoint only.
2. **Interval accumulation**
   Record the sum or integral of all cashflow contributions generated
   between two recordings.
3. **Running accumulation**
   Record the cumulative total from time `0` up to each record time.

I think **interval accumulation** is the most natural default direction
for cashflow.

Why:

- State-rate cashflows are generated continuously across every inner
  step, so summing them over the skipped steps matches the meaning of a
  payment over the period.
- Transition-triggered cashflows are also generated during inner-step
  flow, so interval aggregation is natural there too.
- Scheduled deterministic lump sums can be assigned to the interval that
  contains their event time.
- Users who want a cumulative total can recover it by cumulative summing
  the interval outputs.
- Users who want per-period reporting usually want "cashflow during the
  period", not "instantaneous cashflow at period end".

This means the future cashflow design may reasonably diverge from the
probability design:

- probability recording:
  snapshot at record times
- cashflow recording:
  aggregate between record times

That divergence is not a bug. It reflects that the underlying objects
are different.

## 5. Output shape: named components first, aggregation second

The base object should be **component-wise expected cashflow**, not only
an already-summed total.

Typical users may want separate named streams such as:

- premiums,
- disability annuities,
- death benefits,
- surrender values,
- scheduled bonuses,
- expenses.

The design should preserve those identities in the output and allow
aggregation later. A PyTree-like structure or nested mapping is the
natural shape for that.

The main reason is not just presentation. It is analytical flexibility:

- sometimes the user wants all state-rate cashflows together,
- sometimes only benefit cashflows,
- sometimes everything except premiums,
- sometimes the full total.

The raw result should therefore expose named component streams, and any
aggregation should be a secondary layer.

This does raise an efficiency question: if many components are requested,
the transform should reuse shared intermediate structure where possible
instead of recomputing the same occupancy objects repeatedly. That is an
implementation concern for later, but it is a legitimate reason to keep
the conceptual decomposition explicit now.

---

## 6. Terminal totals and "no-stream" use cases

Some users will not want a full cashflow history at all. They may only
want the total accumulated cashflow, or total present value, at the end
of the solve.

That use case matters because it changes the memory tradeoff.

There are two clean ways to think about it:

### 6.1 Post-process the recorded stream

If the solver returns interval cashflows, then the user can accumulate
them afterward:

- sum over time for an undiscounted terminal total,
- discounted sum for a terminal present value,
- partial sums if needed for diagnostics.

This path is simple and composable. It also keeps one canonical output
shape.

### 6.2 Direct accumulated-output mode

If the user only wants the final total, storing a full time series may
be unnecessary. A future callback mode could maintain running totals in
the scan carry and return only the terminal accumulated values.

That would likely be better when:

- the horizon is long,
- the batch is large,
- many named components are requested,
- the user has no need for time-resolved outputs.

I do not think the design should force an answer yet, but the document
should acknowledge both paths:

- recorded interval streams are the most general base object,
- a direct terminal-accumulation mode may still be worthwhile later for
  efficiency.

---

## 7. Valuation should stay a separate layer by default

Discounting is the most obvious example of a later cashflow transform,
but it is not the only one. Once raw expected cashflow streams exist,
users may want to apply more general post-processing such as:

- discounting,
- inflation or indexation adjustments,
- scenario-specific time weights,
- cumulative sums to terminal values,
- other valuation functionals that act on the generated cashflow stream.

That is why the abstraction should be broader than a "discounting
layer". The better framing is a **valuation layer** applied after
cashflow generation.

Discounting can in principle be embedded directly in each cashflow
function. That is mathematically fine, but it is not obviously the best
default design.

If a valuation weight depends only on clock time, then baking it into
every component function duplicates the same factor everywhere:

- every state-rate callable sees it,
- every transition-lump callable sees it,
- every scheduled-lump callable sees it.

That makes composition and aggregation less clean than necessary.

The better default is:

- compute raw expected cashflow first,
- apply valuation afterward when it depends only on time or on the
  generated cashflow stream,
- reserve in-transform weighting for cases where it genuinely depends on
  state, duration, or transition identity before aggregation.

This fits especially well with interval-recorded outputs. A user can:

- compute raw period cashflows,
- apply period discount factors or other valuation weights,
- accumulate to a terminal value if desired.

That keeps the base cashflow object general and avoids overcommitting
the first design to one valuation convention.

---

## 8. What a future implementation should probably keep separate

Even if the eventual public API is compact, the conceptual separation
should stay visible internally:

- **State-rate transform**
  integrates occupancy against state payment functions.
- **Transition-lump transform**
  integrates occupancy against intensity-weighted transition payment
  functions.
- **Scheduled-event transform**
  evaluates payments on a time schedule against the probability state.
- **Aggregation layer**
  combines named components by user choice.
- **Valuation layer**
  applies later weighting or valuation transforms to generated
  cashflows.

If these are collapsed too early into one opaque "cashflow function",
later questions about valuation, event timing, accumulation mode, or
component reporting become harder to answer cleanly.

---

## 9. Open questions

These points should stay open until implementation planning starts:

1. What exact public API should declare named cashflow components?
2. Should cashflow callbacks use the same `record_every` semantics as
   probability callbacks, or should interval accumulation be the
   explicit default for cashflow?
3. Should deterministic lump sums be restricted to grid-aligned times in
   the first implementation, or should off-grid support be designed in
   from the start?
4. Should a direct "terminal total only" mode exist beside streamed
   outputs?
5. How much built-in aggregation should `jact` provide versus leaving to
   user-side PyTree manipulation?
6. If a deterministic lump occurs exactly on a solver grid point, should
   it see the state before or after that step?

For now, my preferred direction is:

- component-wise named cashflows as the base output,
- scheduled deterministic lumps as a separate time-event mechanism,
- grid-aligned event times first,
- interval accumulation as the leading interpretation of cashflow
  recording,
- valuation treated separately from raw expected cashflow,
- terminal totals supported either by post-processing or later by a
  direct accumulated-output mode.
